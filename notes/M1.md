# M1 — Resampling & small-n inference

*Status: done, July 2026. Code: `quantstats/resample.py`. Demo:
`notebooks/m1_seasonality.ipynb`. Data: Brazilian iron ore exports
(Comex Stat, HS4 2601), monthly, 1997-01 to 2026-06.*

## What the method does

The bootstrap estimates the sampling uncertainty of a statistic without
assuming a distribution. Resample the data with replacement many times,
compute the statistic on each resample, and the spread of those
replicates approximates how much the statistic would vary across
alternative samples you could have drawn. The percentile CI just reads
the middle 95% of the replicates.

Implemented: `bootstrap(data, stat_fn, n_boot, seed)` and
`percentile_ci(replicates, level)`. ~15 lines total. The mechanics are
the easy part.

## Worked example

Question: which calendar months are genuinely above or below trend in
Brazilian iron ore exports?

Pipeline: monthly tonnes → tonnes per day (day-count normalization) →
divide by centered 12-month rolling mean (detrend) → per calendar
month, bootstrap the ~28 seasonal-index values → 95% CI per month.

Result (per-day basis):

- **Below trend, defensible:** Feb (~0.91), Apr (~0.89) — wet season
  in the northern system.
- **Above trend, defensible:** Jul–Oct (~1.06–1.11), peak Sep — dry
  season ramp.
- **No defensible claim:** Jan, Mar, May, Jun, Nov, Dec — intervals
  straddle 1.0. These months *look* patterned in a point-estimate
  chart; the data cannot back it up at n≈28.

## When it lies (all three encountered in this module)

1. **It answers the question as framed, not the question you meant.**
   Bootstrapping raw January tonnes across a series that grew ~10x
   gives a precise CI on a meaningless quantity — the trend dominates
   and 1998 and 2025 are not draws from the same distribution.
   Detrending first (seasonal index) made the question well-posed.
   The CI machinery gave no warning either way.
2. **It puts honest intervals around confounded quantities.** The
   first pass showed Feb at 0.845; roughly half of that was February
   having ~7% fewer days, not weaker demand. Per-day normalization
   moved Feb to ~0.91 and changed the peak month from Jul to Sep.
   Unit corrections can reorder point-estimate rankings.
3. **It assumes independent observations.** The naive bootstrap
   destroys serial correlation by construction. Same-month values from
   adjacent years are close enough to independent for this exercise;
   consecutive months would not be. Block bootstrap handles this —
   known, not yet implemented, will matter for M3/M4.

## What it cannot tell you

- Whether the statistic you chose measures what you care about.
- Anything about values outside the observed range (tails — see M6).
- Whether a pattern will persist; these are descriptive intervals on
  history, not forecasts.

One line: **the bootstrap quantifies sampling uncertainty in whatever
you feed it — it never checks what you fed it.**

## Small-n takeaway

With ~28 observations per month, only 6 of 12 months clear the line,
and the defensible claims are ranges ("Feb runs 5–12% below trend"),
not points. At n=10 (last-decade rerun) the bars widen further and
[fill in: which months survive]. This is the template for every
future setup census: report the interval, name the non-findings.
